Senior Thesis

Research & Analysis



DEVELOPMENT TIMELINE & USABILITY RESPONSE

The first six months of development were spent on creating the block puzzle framework so that new levels could be automatically created by adding lines to a text file. Then, a usability test was sent to approximately 20 participants. The test consisted of one puzzle and aimed to gauge how intuitive the control scheme was. The most commonly reported issues were technical bugs, where the game would not work on certain machines, and UI bugs, where there were many confusing and unintuitive elements, like having to press "Enter" twice to submit text, or not knowing to click the screen to continue the game at various points. After fixing these bugs, including a tutorial, porting it to run in web browsers and improving the game for another few months, another usability test was sent. This time, the primary complaints from participants in the target age group were not related to the usability of the game so much as getting it to run. Many people did not want to download plug-ins to their browser, and found that there was significant effort required to read through the consent form, the tutorials, and all the questionnaires, causing many responses to be technically invalid, though unexpectedly insightful in our analyses.



RESULTS & CONCLUSIONS

After reaching out to approximately 50-100 people, there were only 6 participants who attempted the puzzles, and of those, only 1 successfully played the game and answered all questions. Given that all participants are anonymous, and were given little incentive to spend 15-20 minutes on their responses, this is a risk that was understood from the beginning. While one person is not statistically significant, they did improve from an initial score of 20% on the pre-play questionnaire, to a score of 80% on the post-play questionnaire, which is a positive sign that the game could have helped shape their visualization of the code. There were other participants who did not fill out any surveys or successfully complete the game, but wrote anonymous feedback forms stating that they were confused how to get the game started on their browser, and once again, did not feel comfortable installing the necessary plug-ins. Since I could not assist them directly or else break anonymity, I view this as an interesting conclusion that coincides with results from the usability tests.


Given that the original motivation of this project was to increase "digital fluency" in older adults, perhaps there needs to be more effort in first increasing fluency in being a user of computers before teaching fluency in being a developer. Perhaps a similar analogy could be that one needs to drive a car, fix a car, wash a car, and understand all aspects of owning a car before attempting to learn how to build one for someone else. While understanding how to safely download and install applications or intuitively know where to click on a computer is common for the younger generation, that is not necessarily the case for other age groups. Gamified learning for adults may not be ineffective as an educational tool, but ineffective as a means of distribution. While I cannot draw significant conclusions about how well concepts were learned due to a lack of participation, I think this experience shows that many people in the target age group are still not at a stage of digital fluency that was assumed for participating in this study.


Further work could be done to see if the next generation of older adults (today's 20-30 year old people) would be more receptive to adult gamified learning. More work could also be done on determining adults' interest in technology, and how much time they are willing to spend learning how it works; perhaps its a topic that seems unimportant, or like only people who are experts in math and science can understand it. Regardless, interactive experiences in education have much room to expand, especially across all age groups as more children grow up with technological literacy.
